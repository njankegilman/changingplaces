Who uses Changing Places?

Research undertaken at the University of Dundee concluded that approximately four people in every thousand are potential users of Changing Places. This translates to about 17,000 people in New Zealand. Many of these people are unable to use a standard disability toilet.

These potential users include:

  • people with conditions that may affect their movement, including cerebral palsy, multiple sclerosis, or motor neurone disease
  • people with head injuries, severe spinal injuries, or acquired brain injury
  • people living with stroke
  • people with profound and multiple learning disabilities
  • older people who require assistance

Changing Places are necessary for people who are unable to transfer independently, or who have no sitting balance, or who need a ceiling-mounted hoist to transfer between their wheelchair, toilet, and change table.

Changing Places are necessary for people who are doubly incontinent and who require a change table and shower to be changed with dignity.

Changing Places also provide a privacy screen, to allow the care worker to use the toilet in privacy from the person in their care.

A Changing Place is much more than a disability toilet, it provides sanitary accommodation for people with multiple or complex disabilities who have one or two assistants with them. Without access to a changing facility with all the right equipment, that meets all of their needs, it becomes near-impossible for some people to simply get out and participate in the community. Changing Places open up a world of possibilities for those who need them.

Changing Places should be located at transport hubs, event centres, entertainment venues, and town centres. Key public buildings such as libraries, community halls, airports, and public gardens should consider installing a Changing Place. Even having a single Changing Place in a central downtown location can make it possible for a person with high or complex needs to visit the region.
